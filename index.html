University Senate report raises alarm over ‘critical lack’ of performance, practice space on campus

By Esha Karam and Shea Vance

October 6, 2022

Photo by Genevieve Depke

A University Senate investigation more than half a year in the making has raised major concerns over a “critical lack” of performance and practice spaces at the University.

A report presented at a plenary meeting on Sept. 23 by economics professor John Donaldson, co-chair of the campus planning and physical development committee, cites significantly inadequate rehearsal opportunities for students and an alarming lack of venues for performance groups. It calls for reforms and a University-wide recognition of the “acute” problem.

Donaldson outlined four recommendations for the University based on the report’s findings. The recommendations include a general recognition of the shortage by the administration and the Columbia community as a whole, a new availability of resources, the creation of an electronic reservation system for practice rooms, and starting talks between the University and the schools within Columbia that would be affected by the proposed changes.

“We sincerely believe that [the shortage] is diminishing the experience of any Columbia student, basically, with instincts for artistic or musical expression,” Donaldson said during the plenary.

In addition to a lack of practice space, Columbia is experiencing a shortage of performance spaces, with a limited number of venues available to a considerable number of student performance groups. The report refers to the shortage as “an embarrassment.”

The report comes at a time when the University is grappling with other capacity issues, including course availability and a lack of seats in the dining halls, as conversations surrounding the expansion of the student body have mostly stalled.

“This problem is not going to solve itself, and it’s probably, very likely, only going to get worse if the undergraduate population increases,” Donaldson said.

A multimillion dollar donation from University Trustee Shirley Wang, Business ’93, went toward performance space renovations that will take place over the coming year. Those renovations will include a new stage in the lower level of Lerner Hall. The space is set to open next fall.

The report referenced specific groups of undergraduate and graduate students who are being impacted by the lack of space. Students in the Music Performance Program, School of the Arts program, dance department, Barnard undergraduate theater program, and extracurricular performance groups, groups which together encompass thousands of students, were among the groups mentioned.

Violist and vocalist Oriana Hawley, CC ’25, is a member of the Music Performance Program and the Columbia-Juilliard Program. Hawley, who typically practices around two to three hours a day, told Spectator she is often frustrated by both the quality and quantity of rehearsal, performance, and recording places accessible to her. When Hawley was searching for a recording space last year, she said she faced difficulty booking a room.

“They have that one space, Dodge 112, that I could use at very strange times, like earlier in the morning and late at night,” Hawley said.

As a Columbia-Juilliard student, Hawley has access to additional rooms, such as one in the basement of Hogan Hall. However, other shared practice spaces are open to any Columbia-affiliated students.

“Because there are practice rooms that are available to everyone, in Schapiro, those are very usually booked,” Hawley said. “There are usually people waiting outside at busy times.”

Across East Campus and Schapiro, there are 11 practice rooms available to all University affiliates on campus. If the 500 students in the Music Performance Program were to be guaranteed practice rooms for a maximum of two hours a week, 12 practice rooms would need to be devoted to only those students. However, students in the Music Performance Program currently compete for the same spaces as other bigger theater and performance groups and individual students.

Student theater groups face similar challenges when booking rehearsal and performance spaces on campus.

There are currently 13 different theater groups officially recognized by the Columbia University Performing Arts League who are putting on upwards of 17 shows this semester. Those undergraduate groups primarily use three performance spaces: Lerner’s Austin E. Quigley Black Box Theatre, Barnard’s Glicker-Milstein Theatre, and Roone Arledge Auditorium. While the Glicker-Milstein Theatre is fully funded by the Barnard Theatre Department, student groups must pay a fee to access technicians and lighting for the other two performance spaces.

Miranda Paiz, BC ’25, president of the Columbia University Players, described the semesterly scramble to book spaces.

“There are so many people in those shows, and all of those shows have full casts that need to rehearse all at the same time, essentially,” Paiz said. “And so we all are kind of competing for the exact same spaces.”

The limited performance spaces are often booked to capacity, and the GMT, the cheapest space, had two shows run on the same weekend last fall.

Student groups face difficulties not only with scheduling performances but also with scheduling rehearsal spaces.

Caroline Egler, BC ’24, president of the King’s Crown Shakespeare Troupe and director of one of this semester’s two Columbia Musical Theater Society productions, said the groups have faced difficulties finding spaces to rehearse.

On one occasion, CMTS, which requires a piano for rehearsals, was assigned to an East Campus lounge for rehearsal after Columbia’s Event Management System canceled and rebooked their request. However, the group was kicked out mid-rehearsal because EMS “doesn’t have access to an EC lounge,” Egler said.

“The spaces that we do have are so overused that it just becomes impossible to rely on them,” Egler said.

Paiz also commented on the same issue that the music performance program students face: a lack of priority access to performance spaces that are designed for artistic use.

“At the end of the day, the problem is also that there’s nothing that’s dedicated for theater groups,” Paiz said. “Even rooms like the [Lerner] Broadway room, which you think would be … exclusively for dance and theater groups, people, anyone can book it. And so there’s no real regulation.”

One of the report’s suggestions is to implement an electronic reservation system that would prioritize performance students or performance groups who have a unique need for practice spaces. This solution would more effectively make use of existing practice spaces, the report said.

Paiz and Egler, who have both taken classes in Barnard’s theater department, also note that rehearsal spaces for theater students are limited. All 300 students in the department—approximately 120 to 150 of whom take classes each semester—have only one classroom, Milbank 229, for all their rehearsal needs.

“It was virtually impossible … there’s always classes in there, there’s always people rehearsing,” Egler said, of finding and booking a time to rehearse with peers from their directing class. “People book like two months in advance.”

For master’s of fine arts students in the School of the Arts, rehearsal and classroom spaces are widely dispersed between 115th and 133rd Streets, meaning transportation can be an issue for students. These spaces are also difficult to book and are often occupied. Valeria Contreras, a member of the campus planning committee, co-chair of the student affairs committee, and a master’s student at the SoA, commented on the shortage experienced by graduate students during the senate meeting.

“At this moment, grad students in the School of the Arts don’t really feel that there’s an adequate level of support in the number of spaces or the resources that we have access to,” Contreras said.

The report also comments on the shortcomings of the design of Manhattanville’s Lenfest Center for the Arts, which opened in 2017. One exhibition space has flooring ill-suited for dance performances, while Lenfest’s Black Box Theater lacks storage space for sets, the report says.

“It appears as though the spaces were designed without input from those who would use them,” the report says.

In addition to outlining the potential improvements to the student experience, the committee recognized an incentive for the University to bolster its reputation.

“Many other peer institutions have already invested in their artistic community as part of their own campus planning and strategic priorities, and Columbia University is already really behind,” Contreras said at the senate meeting. “We must prioritize the arts to remain competitive in the future.”

The committee’s presentation emphasized the urgency of the identified shortage.

“The status quo is really not enough, and there’s a strong need and demand to find solutions that demonstrate a commitment to build community and wellness on campus,” Contreras said.
